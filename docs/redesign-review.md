## What I changed, and why

### 1. Your story now has a section, not a sentence

You have an unusual journey — Citi/UBS/RBS in London → CBA in Sydney → leaving corporate in 2024 to coach kids' soccer and start a brokerage named after the team. That's *interesting*. On the current site it's a tagline. Treating it as a proper section ("The long way round to becoming your broker") gives you:

- A reason for someone to trust you over a 23-year-old at a franchise broker
- Context that explains why expats specifically should call you (you've been one)
- A genuinely warm, human moment in the middle of an otherwise transactional journey

This is also where most of your photo assets earn their keep — see the photo plan below.

### 2. Voice is warmer and more conversational

Your LinkedIn voice is genuinely good — observational, dry, never salesy. The current site is closer to corporate-broker copy. I've pulled the new copy toward how you actually write: short sentences, occasional self-deprecation, plain English. Examples:

- *"Wombats are steady, friendly, and stubbornly determined. That's the company I wanted to build."*
- *"I have access to 40+ lenders and, more importantly, a working memory of which lender will actually look kindly on your specific situation."*
- *"Forms, lender liaison, follow-ups. You sign things; I chase everything else."*

This is the voice that will hold up under paid traffic. People click ads sceptically; warm, specific copy converts where polished-corporate copy bounces.

### 3. Process section is now genuinely four-step, not a vague pitch

"Discovery call → Strategy → Application → Settlement & beyond" with one tight sentence each. Critically, step 4 makes a promise that competitors won't: *"I don't disappear at settlement."* Annual reviews are a real differentiator and you should claim it.

### 4. Reviews use full names + segment tags + star ratings + the Google badge

Your existing testimonials are good. They get more credible when:

- The reviewer is identified by a segment tag (*"First home buyer"*, *"Expats from London"*, *"Refinance client"*) — this lets a visitor self-identify
- Specific numbers appear (*"$340/month"*) — vague praise is treated as fluff; specifics are treated as signal
- A "5.0 on Google · verified clients" badge sits next to the section header

I wrote two new testimonial drafts as placeholders styled after your real Andrew quote. **Replace these with real reviews** — happy to help you select the strongest 3 from your Google profile if you paste them in.

### 5. Photo strategy: 5 strategic placements, not 50

You said you don't want the site littered with photos. Agreed. The redesign uses **5 photo slots** (plus 3 *optional* client moment slots). Each one has a specific job — see the next section.

### 6. Typography upgrade

The current site reads as competent but generic. The redesign uses **Fraunces** (a beautiful, slightly literary serif) for display headings paired with **Inter** for body. Fraunces gives you warmth and personality without sacrificing professionalism — it's the kind of typeface a thoughtful financial newsletter or a good architect would use, not a high-street bank. This is your "modern, slightly quirky" brand pillar showing up in the typography itself.

(Kollektif from your brand guide remains the fallback. If you want pure brand-guide compliance, swap Fraunces out — but I'd push you to keep it. It's a meaningful upgrade.)

### 7. Visual rhythm

Six distinct section "moods" so the page doesn't feel monotonous:

1. Hero — light, airy, confident
2. Marquee strip — dark navy, kinetic
3. Story — soft off-white, editorial
4. Who I help — clean white grid
5. Process — full navy, geometric
6. Reviews — light, social-proof heavy
7. Tools — pale blue accent
8. Podcast/articles — white with imagery
9. Closing CTA — full navy with portrait
10. Footer — deep navy

Each transition does work. None of the sections feel like the previous one.

## Photo plan: 5 essentials + 3 optional

You have a folder of property photos and a folder of yourself. Here's exactly what I'd use, where, and why. Think of these as briefs, not literal matches — pick whichever shot in your library best hits the brief.

### `HERO` — the front door (most important shot on the site)
**Brief:** Tom in considered, warm-but-professional environment. Slightly off-camera or in conversation pose — *not* a stiff headshot. Natural light. Suit jacket, no tie (the brand says "modern, friendly, not corporate"). Ideally shot at home / café / Sydney exterior — somewhere that hints at the human, not a studio backdrop.
**Why:** This is the photo that decides whether someone reads the rest of the page. It needs to say "competent and warm" in one image. Avoid: arms-crossed, fake smile, plain white background, studio lighting.

### `STORY-A` — corporate era
**Brief:** Older photo of you from your banking days (Citi/UBS/RBS) **OR** a more recent shot in a business setting that nods to that life. If no archive shot exists, use a strong present-day photo of you at a desk / with screens / in a suit.
**Why:** Visually anchors the "20 years in institutional banking" claim. Doesn't need to be glamorous — just needs to communicate *that* world.

### `STORY-B` — the now
**Brief:** Tom in Beecroft / Sydney suburban context. Casual. Family / community feel. Could be the school ribbon-cutting–style shot, parkrun, caravan trip, walking the streets near home, coaching the soccer team. The point: he lives where his clients live.
**Why:** This is the photo that makes the "two sides of the desk" story land. Without it, the story reads as marketing copy. With it, it reads as biography.

### `PODCAST` — the studio
**Brief:** Tom recording, ideally a wide shot showing the mic / setup / second chair. Or a still from an interview with a guest. Should communicate: this is a real podcast, not a vanity project.
**Why:** The podcast is genuinely a credibility asset. Show it being made.

### `CTA` — the closer
**Brief:** Warm, approachable Tom. Different mood from the hero — more candid. Smiling, mid-laugh, on a phone call, or coffee in hand. The "you'd happily have a coffee with this guy" feeling.
**Why:** This is the last photo before the booking button. It needs to do emotional work, not informational work.

### Optional: `CLIENT-A`, `CLIENT-B`, `CLIENT-C`
**Brief:** Handover/keys moments with consenting clients, OR property exteriors of homes recent clients bought (anonymised, no addresses), OR Sydney streetscapes that fit the audience.
**Why:** Adds a genuine "real outcomes" layer near the testimonials. **Skip entirely if you don't have authentic shots — bad client photos are worse than none.** Stock photography here would actively hurt the brand.

### What I'd specifically *not* do

- No photos of cash, keys held against blue skies, jumping families, or any of the visual cliché of mortgage marketing. The brand guide explicitly warns against stock-photo aesthetics.
- No more than one Tom-portrait per "screen" of scrolling. Five photos of you across the entire page is the upper limit.
- No headshots of you against gradient or solid colour backgrounds. The brand calls for "professional, warm, relatable" — that means a real environment.

## Conversion / paid-marketing readiness

A few things I tightened with paid traffic in mind:

- **Single primary CTA** repeated in 4 places: hero, story end, process end, final CTA section. All point to the same Calendly link. No competing CTAs in the same viewport.
- **Trust strip directly under hero** — Google rating + experience + lender count. Cold paid traffic needs immediate credibility.
- **"No cost to you"** stated explicitly in the process section. This is the single most common buyer objection to brokers and you should address it before they ask.
- **Phone number visible everywhere** — header, footer, final CTA. Some clients will always prefer to phone first; don't make them hunt.
- **Mobile-first layout** — every section stacks cleanly on mobile, the marquee remains readable, the story photos sit naturally. Most paid traffic will be mobile.

## What I'd push back on

One honest note: paid marketing for mortgage brokers is a brutal channel. CPCs in Sydney for *"mortgage broker"* keywords are high, click intent is mixed, and the major aggregators (Aussie, Lendi, Mortgage Choice) outspend you 100:1. Before scaling paid hard, I'd want to see:

- Strong organic intent signals (your podcast and LinkedIn already give you these — keep investing)
- Tight tracking back to actual settled loans, not just bookings
- A clear answer to: *what's my cost-per-settlement, and is it lower than my organic CAC?*

Your existing organic strategy (LinkedIn weekly, podcast, newsletter, Calendly) is genuinely working and is the cheapest channel you have. Paid should *amplify* that, not replace it. Worth budgeting paid as a 20–30% incremental spend rather than the centerpiece, at least to start.
